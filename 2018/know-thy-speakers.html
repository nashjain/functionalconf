What has been your best moment or highlight working with FP?

MICHAEL SNOYMAN

MICHAEL SNOYMAN Vice President of Engineering @ FP Complete

What got you into Functional Programming (FP)?
I'd been programming Java and other languages for years, and as time went on I realized my productivity was holding me back. I worked with more lightweight tools, but never felt comfortable shipping to customers, knowing that I couldn't be confident in code quality. FP for me is the best of both worlds: a highly productive set of practices that ensure a higher quality bar. And great performance is a wonderful perk!

What has been your best moment or highlight working with FP?
This may sound surprising, but it's probably refactoring. I strongly believe that we overoptimize for first implementation of code, and forget that we spend most of our time maintaining existing code. Requirements change, and old architecture decisions no longer make sense. Strongly typed functional programming allows a level of refactoring, rearchitecting, and rewriting that I would never achieve in less strongly typed solutions. The compiler is my ally.

All the mainstream programming languages are adding functional programming features. Most new languages and frameworks are strongly influenced by FP. What is your advice to object oriented programmers?
Learn Haskell, even if you aren't planning on using it. You can learn FP principles in Javascript or other hybrid languages. But being forced to do things in a purely FP language will up your skills in a way that hybrid languages rarely grant.

You are presenting - Applied Haskell Workshop / Functional Programming for the Long Haul - at the conference. What are some of the key takeaways from your sessions?
Functional Programming for the Long Haul

  • How we make decisions about programming languages, libraries, and frameworks today
  • Where I think these methods fall short
  • Why functional programming isn't getting the credit it deserves
  • Improvements to our evaluation strategies

Applied Haskell Workshop:

  • How to build real world application with Haskell
  • How to choose and understand libraries
  • Evaluation order in Haskell
  • Proper exception handling, including async exceptions

ADITYA ATHALYE

ADITYA ATHALYE Programmer @ Vayana Network

What got you into Functional Programming (FP)?
At my first software engineering job (with Helpshift.com), I discovered that some problem domains are object hierarchies, some are pipelines, some are procedural, and in every case the State is the Frenemy. So, to the extent that I try to reduce statefulness of my code (i.e. stave off insanity and remain functional), I might agree to be labeled a “Functional” programmer.

What has been your best moment or highlight working with FP?
In the foggy mists of time, 70-odd pages into solving SICP, I realized I'd already implemented a whole lot of logic without once setting a variable. Then it took me a few years to actually understand the implications of that. And years more to develop some intuition for when to keep The State at bay, and when to gingerly embrace it.
While at it, I discovered “FP” things in “OO” lands, and “OO” things in “FP” lands. Apparently one can do excellent “object oriented” programming using Clojure without knowing a single classical OO design pattern, process hundreds of GBs of logs with purely functional bash pipelines, learn to safely orchestrate complex infrastructure with Ansible (procedural, but idempotent), and discover new and clever ways to shoot oneself in the foot with all of these (after the foot is shot, of course).
Well, well, well I suppose if I zoom out enough, the whole thing will start looking like a best moment?

All the mainstream programming languages are adding functional programming features. Most new languages and frameworks are strongly influenced by FP. What is your advice to object orientated programmers?
All of the following, IMHO, and YMMV:
Whatever one's persuasion re: language, State is the Frenemy. The core challenge of all programming is how to cause only the intended effects, only in predictable ways. And here's the kicker… If one really stares it in the face, all code is inherently stateful, because of the many unwritten intentions and unsaid assumptions that don't go with it (but which are so essential to its correct working today, and to its correct transmutation over time). I.e. we're already attempting the impossible.
So, I don't think there's any one absolute reality called “object oriented” programming; nor one called “functional” programming.
I do think, however, that there's purpose-appropriate problem-solving. And that the trickiest problems that manifest in our code do not fundamentally originate in language capability, but through misunderstanding the actual problem at hand and/or our inability to solve it correctly (under natural constraints of money, mindspace, and markets). Also, I believe, these problems are due to our recently-acquired penchant for seeking technological solutions to whatever may be the problem (real or imagined). Said more tongue-in-cheek; whether mainstream or niche, object oriented or functional; if deleted code is good code, then averted code is great code.
Concurrent to these comments; one can't beat the laws of Physics. Entropy will always surprise us in ways we cannot imagine or plan for. I feel that the humility and watchfulness that come through acquiring this perspective are far better bedfellows than the paradigms and preconceptions that come bundled with Language Oriented Problem Solving (or LOPSided programming).

You are presenting- Clojure By Example: What could one do with just a bit of Clojure?- at the conference. What are some of the key takeaways from your session?
Steal good ideas from wherever you find them (legally, of course), and figure out how to apply them appropriately in your context. I'd declare my session a success if it gets people to seriously wonder:

  • Could there be value to modeling domain entities as immutable data structures?
  • Of transforming and manipulating said entities with “pure” functions?, and
  • What kind of leverage can one derive from a “Live”/Interactive programming workflow?

Mainly the “hallway track” at lunch and coffee, where I get to gab with old friends, make new ones, and serendipitously find myself part of interesting conversations.

What got you into Functional Programming (FP)?
My first job had me writing ETL jobs on Apache Spark, and my company encouraged me to learn Scala to better deal with its DSL. Over the years, I got into the deeper concepts of FP to better understand the math and design patterns behind it, and here I am, 4 years later, still feeling that there is a world of concepts yet to be uncovered.

What has been your best moment or highlight working with FP?
FP has not been the easiest to grasp so whenever I cross the ‘Great Filter' when it comes to a new concept is always a significant day for me. Code just starts looking different each time you truly grasp the idea behind it. Over the years, I've seen so many problems in software design and programming that have been solved so elegantly by FP which only vindicates my blind trust in spending years poring through books and literature. My best moment would probably be if I can encourage at least one person to go back after my talk and dedicate themselves to learning FP, because FP is the past, present and future of programming.

All the mainstream programming languages are adding functional programming features. Most new languages and frameworks are strongly influenced by FP. What is your advice to object oriented programmers?
My very first memory of starting to work in FP was how much I had to unlearn the OOP background I had from college. As with anything in life, approach FP with the eagerness of a student who knows nothing, free of biases and you'll come out gaining a lot more than you expected. It is very common to see programmers writing in FP languages in a very non-FP paradigm. A little effort in understanding the nuances and what additional capabilities a language offers goes a long way in improving productivity. I don't like to get into a debate on which language is better. The more languages you come across, the more you understand the strengths of each and the wisdom to know when to use them.

You are presenting - Algebraic Data Types: Just my kinda Type - at the conference. What are some of the key takeaways from your session?
My talk is about a very fundamental idea in designing data structures which is almost ingrained in us over years of working, to the point that it is almost a subconscious factor while coding. My aim is to highlight the nuances and to appreciate the mathematical rigour behind it so that it becomes a more conscious choice the next time you think about designing domain classes or a library. A proper foundation goes a long way into the lifecycle of software development, and I hope people can take away some useful ideas.

DEBASISH GHOSH

DEBASISH GHOSH Principal Software Engineer @ Lightbend Inc

What got you into FP ?
I discovered Scala way back around 2008 when Martin first introduced the language. Coming from a Java background, I was impressed with the succinctness of the language. Till then I was looking at Scala as a better Java and was learning some of the concepts of algebraic data types, pattern matching, traits etc. Many of the FP concepts like higher order functions were new to me and I suddenly found myself using them in some of my personal projects. And not only did the code become more concise, it was more expressive too. One of the foundational papers that I read during this time was Why Functional Programming Matters by John Hughes that clearly demonstrated all the goodness that FP brings to your programming world. It was also around this time I discovered quite a lot of resources on FP and I specifically remember 1 paper by Graham Hutton on folding of data structures that had a lasting impact on me. At around the same time I came across a library in Scala named “scalaz” which was basically Haskell in Scala. I think scalaz was possibly the most dominant impact that inspired me to do FP in Scala. In course of time I got into Haskell, Idris etc. and now I can say that I love FP.

What has been your best moment or highlight working with FP ?
I think when I could use the principles of FP on large projects implementing real world domain models, I felt good. In theory FP makes you write modular code that are easier to reason about than imperative code. But experiencing it in a real world project was an Aha! moment for me.

All the mainstream programming languages are adding functional programming features. Most new languages and frameworks are strongly influenced by FP. What is your advice to object oriented programmers?
John Carmac once mentioned on twitter that “Sometimes, the elegant implementation is just a function. Not a method. Not a class. Not a framework. Just a function.” This will be my advice as well - keep your eyes open and choose the best tool for your implementation.

You are presenting- Power of Functions in a Typed World on the JVM / Managing Effects in Domain Models - The Algebraic Way - at the conference. What are some of the key takeaways from your sessions?
The first session is a tutorial targeted mostly towards the beginners of FP. I will try to demonstrate how FP in a statically typed language makes your program more expressive, modular, testable and easier to reason about. I will show some samples of real world code that I have implemented using pure FP in Scala. I will start with some theory of what types are, how they form an algebra and how alignment of types leads to function composition.

The second talk is a bit advanced where I will talk about how to handle effects in Scala using algebraic techniques. Side-effects are ubiquitous in a model and FP offers techniques to handle them in a pure and referentially transparent way. Of course you need to execute side-effects when you interact with the run time system. But till then you can program with pure values that offer composability to build larger abstractions out of smaller ones. I will discuss some patterns and techniques that are used in the wild today.

HARENDRA KUMAR


HARENDRA KUMAR Engineer @ Composewell Technologies Private Limited

What got you into Functional Programming (FP)?
I have worked on hard core systems for more than a decade. Imagine a large and complex C/C++ codebase for system's software with millions of lines of code, a new engineer taking more than a year to get the head or tail of it, and a new release taking more than a year to deliver. I worked towards modularizing and simplifying the spaghetti code to solve some of these problems. I left my job and started looking for ways to program better. Later, I figured that I was trying to reinvent a crude functional programming paradigm. I found that there is a whole new world of functional programming out there, it is highly evolved, a huge amount of research and engineering effort has gone into it. However, almost no one I worked with would ever have heard of the Haskell programming language, if you say the name Haskell it is sure to be confused with Pascal. For me it was like a dream suddenly coming true. I found that Haskell is the epitome of pure functional programming, something that I could only imagine earlier.
Now I am working towards bringing the composability and maintainability of Haskell to high performance systems. The larger goal is, whether you are writing a “hello world” or a distributed supercomputing program, you program in exactly the same way, it should be a child's play. I believe it is achievable with Haskell with some effort. Streamly is a Haskell library towards that goal, it makes non-concurrent and concurrent programming indistinguishable.

What has been your best moment or highlight working with FP?
When starting with Haskell I wrote a small program with a few hundred lines of code. After I was finished compiling it, I ran it and it worked flawlessly, while iterating on this program I could not find a single bug after compilation. I do not claim that you can never have a bug, but it is highly robust against language induced bugs because of a strong static type system. The compiler often reveals bugs in your thinking when the types do not fit in. When you are writing a C program the worst moment is before you try to run your program and you cross your fingers before shipping, with Haskell you get the shivers before you compile but you are mostly in peace after that. You can refactor a large Haskell code base fearlessly without worrying about correctness after the refactor. Try refactoring a large C code base, and experience the hell, well, only the brave can even think about doing it.

All the mainstream programming languages are adding functional programming features. Most new languages and frameworks are strongly influenced by FP. What is your advice to object orientated programmers?
Object oriented model is the only way to get some sanity in the imperative programming model. However, the problems with imperative model are deeply rooted. Managing state in a large complex software becomes very challenging because it is hard to track how and where state gets modified. It is easy to pass references but harder to find all the places where you can modify the contents of some memory. Adele Goldberg said, “In Smalltalk, everything happens somewhere else.” That is exactly the problem with imperative and object oriented programming. Pure functional programming just inverts the model, no function can ever change any memory outside it, the only way to communicate is through explicit output of the function. State management is explicit and carefully designed and therefore much easier to track what is going on and harder to make a mess. The best thing it brings is composablity, you can fearlessly put blocks together, they have much fewer and disciplined interactions with the rest of the code.

You are presenting- High Performance Haskell - at the conference. What are some of the key takeaways from your session?
If Haskell is so good, what is the catch? The functional model is dual (complementary) to the imperative model. The problems are also of the dual nature. When you refactor a C code base, it is bound to break the correctness of the program but performance is more or less unaffected. In Haskell it is the reverse, correctness of the program is maintained after a refactor but getting to the same performance levels may need some work. I have had a first hand experience of it during the development of Streamly. The key takeaways from this session are, (1) Haskell can compete with C with some work, (2) common pitfalls impacting performance, (3) ways to improve performance with some examples from my experience, (4) tools that may help when working on performance optimization.

On bootcamp day, I would recommend Introduction to Haskell syntax and tools, Starting Data61 Functional Programming Course, as well as Property Based Testing for beginners. For main conference - keynote for inspiration on FP, “Refresh-driven” development with Haskell & Elm, Using Haskell to build a performant GraphQL to SQL compiler, and Many Ways to Concur sound exciting.

All the mainstream programming languages are adding functional programming features. Most new languages and frameworks are strongly influenced by FP. What is your advice to object oriented programmers?
Start small. When it comes down to it, functional programming is just passing arguments to functions and eschewing complicated state and language features. This is something you can do in any language with - or without - FP specific language features. Build a feature with no
mutable variables, write a function that only works with its arguments and doesn't rely on shared state. As you do this more, you will both get better at it and start to pick up on the patterns that provide you the most value.

You are presenting - Property Based Testing / Hanging on in Quiet Desperation: Time & Programming - at the conference. What are some of the key takeaways from your sessions?
The goal of the property based testing workshop is to give people the tools that they need to have confidence applying property testing in practice. Property testing has the potential to make one of the most significant contributions to reducing bugs and increasing understanding of your code out of any of the easily adoptable FP techniques, however, to those without experience and property testing can be rather challenging to get started with. The workshop aims to
break down that barrier by teaching the mindset required to identify and implement useful properties, as well as showing some repeatable patterns that can be followed in almost every situation.

Hanging on in Quiet Desperation: Time & Programming is a retrospective look at FP as a tool for working with time as a first class entity in our systems. It looks at a how controlling time gives us the ability to build programs that are more powerful, easier to understand and work better than when we ignore time.
